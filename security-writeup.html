What makes a good security write‑up?

Quick, readable notes you can reference during reviews or interviews.

Shared Responsibility Model (SRM)

  • Defines which security tasks are handled by the cloud provider vs the customer; varies by hosting model: SaaS, PaaS, IaaS, on‑prem.
  • Moving to the cloud shifts some responsibilities to the provider; others stay with the customer.
  • Always the customer’s responsibility: information/data, devices (mobile/PC), accounts & identities.
  • On‑prem datacenter: customer handles everything from physical security to data encryption.
  • IaaS: most customer management. Provider offers infrastructure; customer handles OS, network controls, apps, and data protection.
  • PaaS: provider manages hardware/OS; customer focuses on applications and data.
  • SaaS: least customer management. Provider manages almost everything except data, devices, accounts, and identities.
  • Benefit: clarity on who owns which risks and controls.

Defense in Depth (DiD)

  • Layered security rather than a single perimeter.
  • Common layers: physical; identity & access; perimeter (e.g., DDoS protection); network; compute; application; data.
  • Goals follow CIA:
    • Confidentiality — protect sensitive data (passwords, PII, financials).
    • Integrity — keep data accurate and unaltered.
    • Availability — ensure authorized users have timely access.

Zero Trust Model

  • Assumes a hostile network by default — trust no one, verify everything.
  • Principles: verify explicitly; least‑privilege access (JIT, JEA, data protection); assume breach (segment by network/user/device/app; prefer encryption).
  • Pillars: identities, devices, applications, data, infrastructure, networks.

Encryption & Hashing

  • Encryption — makes data unreadable to unauthorized viewers; requires a key to decrypt.
  • Types: symmetric (same key for enc/dec) and asymmetric (public/private pair). Asymmetric is common for HTTPS and digital signatures.
  • States of data: at rest (storage), in transit (across networks), in use (memory/CPU).
  • Hashing — one‑way transformation to a fixed‑length value; no keys, not reversible; often used for password storage.

GRC (Governance, Risk, Compliance)

  • Governance — rules, practices, and processes to direct and control the org.
  • Risk — identify, assess, and respond to threats (internal & external).
  • Compliance — laws & regulations that dictate protections, processes, and penalties.
  • Related concepts: data residency, data sovereignty, data privacy.
